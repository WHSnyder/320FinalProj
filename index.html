Predicting Success in March Madness from Regular Season Metrics

by John Taormina, Jared Welsh, Will Snyder

Goal: Compare the Influence of Regular Season Performance and Team Experience on Team Success in March Madness.¶


Purpose:¶


Why study college basketball statistics? How much insight could you really get from a complex set of variables all influenced by the interactions of the human body, the general state of the sport over time, player mentality, team politics and morale, and team experience? This a very valid question. One could say that you shouldn't even attempt to fully comprehend the seemingly quantum-mechanics-level complexity of these variables as they play out between players. Instead, you should attempt to distinguish between the influence of bigger picture metrics at the team level.

A logical starting point for determining the traits that make or break a team would be relating these traits to team success in March Madness. March Madness is a best-of-one tournament that moves at breakneck speed. The mental weight and intensity of March Madness implies the importance of one particular dynamic: objective sense of a team's skill vs. its players' experience. A skilled team that did well in the regular season has a reasonable chance of success in the playoffs. This is undoubtadly true, but the experience of that team implies cohesion and leadership: crucial traits for weathering the playoff storm.

This tutorial does not aim to create a money-printing bracket generator. We will test simple models with simple configurations. The greatest strength of this tutorial is the extensively documented scraping process. We account for minor data inconsistencies, issues you might find when requesting large amounts of data via script, campatibility issues between systems, parsing HTLM DOM trees directly, and more.

Data Overview¶

Our data consists of:

  1. SRS - Simple Rating System: #average point differential of all regular season games played/#average differential of all opponents.

    a. A thorough walkthrough of the logic behind this ranking system can be found here

  2. SOS - Strength of Schedule: #average point differential of opponents.

  3. OSRS, DSRS: Offensive and Defensive Variations on SRS

  4. Player Experience: sum over top ten players on team{ player's rank on team weight SRS/100 }

  5. Raw Player Experience: sum over top ten players on team{ player's rank on team * weight }

    a. The weight is defined by this mapping: Freshman: 1.0, Sophomore: 1.1, Junior: 1.2, Senior: 1.3.
    b. Teammates are ranked relative to eachother by points + rebounds + assists

  6. Team Wins: Simply the number of wins earned by a team in the playoffs. Its exact interpreation is detailed before the modeling process.


Regular season team data (1-3) are all stored in a single table per year.


Both Player Experience features are drawn from roster data stored in a single table per team per year (hence the lengthy scraping time...). We offer CSV files that already contain this data in the repo root in case you do not wish wait 5-10 minutes.


Team Wins is drawn from brackets stored as DOM trees. The tournament scraping section goes into much greater detail.


These metrics are defined consistently throughout the years on www.sports-reference.com. We could not find many examples of missing data either. Sports-reference is a great website for sports statistics and they have much more to offer than college basketball.

Part B: Scraping Tournament Data per Year per Team¶

Next we must iterate through each year and team to get records of each tournament (March Madness). We then return a dictionary linking teams to their tournament wins for each year (a Pandas column). This data is not in a table on sports-reference. It is stored as a tree (tournament bracket) of div elements. This is relatively easy to traverse. We determine the # of wins a team got for that year by simply counting the # of times a team's name appears in the tree. The comments below go into greater detail on the HTML DOM parsing process.

In [11]:
# The function first creates an empty list that will hold the names that appear in the bracket.
# Next, we get the url and remove comments. When then find the div item that houses the bracket. From there
# we look at the children of the div, skipping every other element because they aren't the brackets.
# There are two different types of brackets, conference brackets and the final four. Because the final four
# bracket has a different id, we need to account for that in the first line. From there we continue into the 
# html until we find the element that houses the team names. From there we get all of the team names and replace
# them with their ratings counterpart, to ensure name standardization. finally we return the counter representation
# of our list, giving us a dictionary with team names and their tourney wins.

def get_tourney_wins(year):
    playoff_teams = []
    
    url = 'https://www.sports-reference.com/cbb/postseason/'+str(year)+'-ncaa.html'
    req = requests.get(url)
    soup = BeautifulSoup(re.sub("<!--|-->", "", req.text), 'html.parser')
    
    b = soup.find('div', {'id' : 'brackets'})
    
    for conf in list(b.children)[1::2]:
        cls = 'team4' if 'national' in conf.get('id') else 'team16'
        bracket = conf.find('div', {'class': cls})
        rnds = bracket.findAll('div', {'class': 'round'})
        for rnd in rnds:
            divlist = filter(lambda a: 'schools' in a['href'], rnd.findAll('a'))
            playoff_teams += [fix_name(a) for a in divlist]
    
    
    return dict(Counter(playoff_teams))

This function creates scrapes each team's SRS, SOS, DSRS, and OSRS for each year into a dataframe. It also calls get_tourney_wins, and merges the returned dataframe with the aforementioed performance dataframe. There are numerous quirks to this method, please see the comments for more detail.

# This function accepts a year, which is used in creating the url for the data we are trying to access.
# In this function we first call the get_tourney_wins function.
# This function is called first because we only care about teams that made it to the tournament.
# The ratings page has all teams that competed that year. In order to save time we remove all teams

def generate_df(year):
    tourney_wins = get_tourney_wins(year)
    playoff_teams = tourney_wins.keys()
    
    # Next, we use requests to get the html page containing the ratings for each year. We remove the comments 
    # from the document to make parsing easier. We then get the table tag, which has all the schools ratings.
    # We create a dataframe from the table. Every 20 entries on the website, a new table is used/ 
    # Due to the way that the data is laid out on the website, multiple dataframes are actually created
    # when calling read_html. In order to combine these dataframes, we use concat. We also need to droplevel
    # to fix the column names. If we do not use this, the column names are set as the first row of data.

    req = requests.get('https://www.sports-reference.com/cbb/seasons/'+str(year)+'-ratings.html')
    soup = BeautifulSoup(re.sub("<!--|-->", "", req.text), 'html.parser')
    table = soup.findAll('table')
    s_df = pd.read_html(str(table))
    
    # Now that the dataframe is beginning to take shape, we remove all columns that aren't needed. From there,
    # we remove all rows that do not contain data. Because of the way the table is laid out on the website,
    # the column names appear multiple times as data. We remove this and reset the index.
    # From there we add new columns used for managing the year, flags to drop non-playoff teams and additionally
    # prep column filled out later.
    
    temp_df = (pd.concat(s_df))
    temp_df.columns = temp_df.columns.droplevel()
    temp_df.reset_index(drop=True, inplace=True)
    
    standings_df = temp_df[['School', 'SRS', 'SOS', 'OSRS', 'DSRS']].copy()
    standings_df = standings_df[~standings_df.SRS.str.contains("SRS")]
    standings_df.dropna(inplace=True)
    
    standings_df['EXP'] = 0.0
    standings_df['year'] = year
    standings_df['playoff'] = False
    standings_df['t_wins'] = 0
    
    # From here we change the column type to a numerical value for later use and update the tournament wins for
    # each team. We then drop all non-tournament teams using the column we created and then remove that column.
    
    standings_df.SRS = standings_df.SRS.astype(float)
    standings_df.OSRS = standings_df.OSRS.astype(float)
    standings_df.DSRS = standings_df.DSRS.astype(float)
    standings_df.SOS = standings_df.SOS.astype(float)
    
    
    
    for indx, row in standings_df.iterrows():
        if row['School'] in playoff_teams:
            wins = tourney_wins[row['School']]
            standings_df.loc[indx, 'playoff'] = True
            standings_df.loc[indx, 't_wins'] =  wins - 1 if wins < 5 else wins - 2 #Winning team listed one more time in tree.
    
    
    standings_df = standings_df[standings_df.playoff].reset_index(drop=True)
    del standings_df['playoff']
    return standings_df

Part A: Migrating Team Roster Data from CSV to Pandas¶

Now that we've got our roster data in CSV files, we need to store it in a Pandas dataframe. Once we've done this, for each team for each year, we can calculate total player experience.

Total player experience is a crucial metric. Players are by default ranked by their row ordering in the scraped tables. The player experience metric multplies this rank by a weight corresponding to the player's year. The wieghts for freshmen through seniors are 1.0 to 1.6 respectively.

If the best players on a team are all seniors, the player experience metric is maximized, since you have skilled players with leadership capabilities and knowledge. If a team's best players are mostly freshmen who have not faced the pressure of playoffs, the experience metric will reflect that.

# This fuction calculates a player experience variable that 
# takes into account the skill of a player, and how old they are (FR, SO, JR, SR)

def calculate_player_experience(year, team):
    df_path = 'rosters' + '/' + str(year) + '/' + team + '.csv'
    df = pd.read_csv(df_path)
    # Weights based on each starter, then bench as a whole player
    start_weight = 10
    class_weights = {'FR': 1.0, 'SO': 1.1, 'JR': 1.2, 'SR': 1.3}
    player_exp = 0
    df = df.fillna('X')
    
    for index, row in df.iterrows():
        
        if(row['Class'] != 'X'):
            
            player_exp = player_exp + class_weights[row['Class']]*start_weight
            player_exp = round(player_exp, 2) 

            start_weight = start_weight - 1
            
            if start_weight == 0: break

    return(player_exp)

Part C: Preprocessing¶

Now that we have everything in one dataframe, we need to refine our player experience and team performance metrics to work well with linear regression.

First, for each player, we multiply their weighted experience score by their teams SRS. We do this to account for the interaction between player skill and team performance (ie, the best players on the best teams should be further weighted).

Second, one of the problems with looking at tournament wins is that half the number of teams get knocked out at each level. This causes problems because the data becomes skewed towards lower numbers. For example, 32 people have 0 wins, but only 2 people have 4. This causes any linear regression to have bias towards the lower values. In order to avoid this, we perform linear regression on the means of each group, for each variable. For example when plotting, we take the mean of teams with 0 wins in 1984, 1 win in 1984 and so on and so on. This information is stored in a new dataframe, 'df_means.'

Conclusion¶

In conclusion, we saw that raw player experience does not make a strong playoff team. SRS proved to be the dominant metric and in hindsight this seems quite obvious. The best regular season teams usually win. Our data shows that they recieve a marginal boost with more experienced players, but our results are truthfully pretty negligible in that regard. The player_experience_raw feature is not multiplied by SRS and musters a meager correlation of .14. This shows that player experience is simply not that important in and of itself.

Further experiments should account for outliers in all fields. This is very apparent when looking at the regression results for player_exp_raw and DSRS. Sometimes strange things happen to teams or sudden injuries threw the group dynamic off balance. Again, we have not accounted for numerous factors that could affect a college basketball team's performance.

Our definition of player experience could use some work. Our system of weighting by class and multiplying by SRS accounts for experience and team skill but there may be problems within our definition of experience. Maybe our weights should be completely different, although after trying many different configurations, I doubt it's the weights themsleves that cause the problem. More likely we should account for each player's playing time and previous tournament experience. We could also consider the exact effect of accruing more experience. Maybe players who have played in one NCAA Tournament will play much better than those who have not, but a player with 3 tournaments will not play significantly differently than one with 2 (on average of course).
